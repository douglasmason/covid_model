Top-Level Strategy Considerations for COVID-19 Modeling

Douglas Mason[1]

Jun 26, 2020

We can either employ interpretable or non-interpretable models to predict COVID-19 hospitalization rates, but all models will suffer from the flaw that the training data is not truly independent and identically distributed. Contiguous regions will affect each other’s infection growth rates, and all predictions evaluated on past data suffer from the fact that there are correlations in time that may or may not persist into the future.

By relaxing the interpretability constraint, we can employ more complex models like ensemble methods such as random forests or XGBOOST, support vector machines (SVNs), deep neural networks (DNNs), recurrent neural networks (RNNs), long-short-term memory (LSTM) RNNs, and others, but it will be difficult to engender much faith in these models since they will be trained and evaluated on historical data and are likely to be picking up spurious signals that derail their predictions into the actual future. Despite these flaws, non-interpretable models can perform as a reach goal. Their evaluation metrics will be inflated, and this will tell us the upper limit of prediction ability in the interpretable models.

Interpretable models, on the other hand, allow us to use human thought to better understand our predictions and which ones are likely to be accurate. Moreover, we can use these models as a baseline. As we add complexity to our models, we should see our evaluation metrics increase, and we should stop at the maximum complexity we can tolerate. Note that this is similar to addressing the variance-bias trade-off, i.e., regularization to avoid overfitting, but instead of using a predetermined recipe (e.g., L1, L2, or drop-out regularization) to prune our model parameters, we are using domain knowledge about the models and their features to achieve superior results.

  2. Exponential regression with weekly profile (i.e., linear regression in the log space, for example, see Figure 1)
  3. Exponential regression with weekly profile and first-order (and higher) corrections

We expect that as we explore increasingly complex models, we will reach diminishing returns and more susceptibility to flaws in the data, and we will learn the limits of our predictive ability. For example, code and visualizations for exponential regression have already been implemented in an automated manner by one of our collaborators[2] using data from the New York Times and John Hopkins University, and this approach has been successful in predicting hotspots about a week before it hits the news. Moreover, this work has made it possible to correlate growth rates over time to various demographic statistics and infer whether infection or testing rates are increasing (see Figure 1). Is this best that we can reasonably do, or can we do better? We would use this model as our baseline in research and development.

A Few Notes About Universal Curves and Simulation

A common approach in epidemiological research is to fit universal curves to the data, or to run simulations using partial or ordinary differential equations (ODEs). If the universal curve is defined to have a pre-set final state, however, it is very easy to underestimate prediction uncertainty. For example, one could define a universal curve that starts at zero and always predicts the same asymptotic infection rate, say, through a logistic function. Such models will provide zero uncertainty in their asymptotic predictions, but we would never trust these results because they’re predetermined, even if they have high performance metrics such as R2. Many current studies make a similar flaw in their logic, usually by hiding the predetermined asymptote in a complicated formulation. And without robust and varied means of estimating uncertainties, too much confidence can be given to inaccurate estimates of uncertainty even when the asymptote is variable (see Section “Methods for Computing Uncertainty’)

When fitting the data to curves produced by simulation, it is important to understand the simulation’s assumptions thoroughly. For example, a major flaw in the common ODE-based SIR and SEIR[3] epidemiology models is that they model behavior from the perspective of extremely long time scales and at high saturation rates for the population. Meanwhile, we have likely only seen an actual population saturation of a few percent. Moreover, these models are derived from applications in chemistry, and in an effort to reduce the parameters needed, grossly over-simplify the process. For example, in the SIR/SEIR models, a given person can become infected, transmit, and die, all in the same instant. On the other hand, we have supporting evidence that there is an incubation period lasting up to two weeks during which those transmissions are impossible.

Figure 2: Diagram of the delayed Gaussian convolution kernel used to model transitions between states in the epidemiology model

Further elaborations of the SIR/SEIR model add transition states to allow for people to pool into, with the intention to simulate delayed transmission, but these approaches only address part of the problem and should be fundamentally re-thought. Previous work by one of our collaborators, in an effort to identify the impact of shelter-in-place orders as quickly as possible, employs a combined ODE and convolutional approach to achieve the desired effect. The ODE part simply allows the researcher to smoothly transition between different growth rates in the contagious population, and a convolution kernel made from a delayed gaussian is used to simulate delayed transitions to the infectious/hospitalized/deceased populations (see Figure 2). Since the delayed Gaussian has three parameters (delay, width, and area-under-the-curve), this model has more parameters than SIR or SEIR, but it more accurately captures real-world phenomena and with highly interpretable model parameters.

Our collaborator has also developed code to explore the many methods for calculating model uncertainty for these types of models to demonstrate their strengths and weaknesses, and has been able to infer testing and case fatality rates using this approach. Future development can adapt the model to measure not just the onset of shelter-in-place but also other fluctuations in the growth rate as the pandemic progresses.
